Geometric vectors, dot product, cross product, projections, and lines and planes in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

LAW · CH4 · §4.1–§4.5
§4.1 Geometric Vectors
Definitions 4.1–4.3 · Theorems 4.1.1–4.1.5
Vectors in Rⁿ
Definition 4.1 — Geometric Vector
For distinct points A, B in $\mathbb{R}^3$ the geometric vector $\overrightarrow{AB}$ is the directed arrow from tail A to tip B. Two geometric vectors are equal iff they share the same length and direction regardless of location. Point P(x,y,z) is identified with column vector $\mathbf{v} = (x,y,z)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^3$.
Theorem 4.1.1 — Length (Norm)
For $\mathbf{v} = (x, y, z)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^3$:
Euclidean Norm $$\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$$ $$\|\mathbf{0}\| = 0 \quad \text{(zero vector uniquely has length 0)}$$ $$\|a\mathbf{v}\| = |a|\|\mathbf{v}\| \quad \text{for all scalars } a$$
Unit Vector & Normalisation
A vector u with $\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1$ is a unit vector. For nonzero v the unique unit vector in the same direction is:
Normalise $$\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|} \quad \text{(scale factor } \tfrac{1}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}\text{)}$$
Standard Basis / Coordinate Vectors
i = (1,0,0)ᵀ, j = (0,1,0)ᵀ, k = (0,0,1)ᵀ. Any vector: v = xi + yj + zk. More generally in $\mathbb{R}^n$ the standard basis is e₁, …, eₙ.
Theorem 4.1.4 — Vector Between Points & Distance
For $P_1(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and $P_2(x_2,y_2,z_2)$:
Displacement & Distance $$\overrightarrow{P_1P_2} = (x_2-x_1,\; y_2-y_1,\; z_2-z_1)^\top$$ $$\text{dist}(P_1,P_2) = \|\overrightarrow{P_1P_2}\| = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2+(z_2-z_1)^2}$$
Example 4.1.1 / 4.1.3
v = (2,−1,3)ᵀ → $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{14}$.
$P_1(2,-1,3)$, $P_2(1,1,4)$: $\overrightarrow{P_1P_2} = (-1,2,1)^\top$, $\text{dist} = \sqrt{6}$.
Vector Arithmetic Properties
Parallelogram Law & Tip-to-Tail Rule
v + w is the diagonal (same tail) of the parallelogram determined by v and w. Equivalently:
$$\overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{BC} = \overrightarrow{AC} \quad \text{(tip-to-tail)}$$
Vectors can be chained tip-to-tail; the net vector runs from first tail to last tip.
Theorem 4.1.3 — Difference as Vector
If v, w share a common tail A with tips B and C: v − w = CB⃗ (vector from tip of w to tip of v).
Definition 4.2 — Parallel Vectors & Theorem 4.1.5
Nonzero v and w are parallel iff they have the same or opposite direction, i.e. $\mathbf{v} = t\mathbf{w}$ for some scalar $t \neq 0$. (Proved using the scalar-multiple law.)
Scalar Multiple Law
For scalar a and nonzero v:
Length & Direction of av $$\|a\mathbf{v}\| = |a|\|\mathbf{v}\|$$ $a > 0$ → same direction as $\mathbf{v}$; $a < 0$ → opposite direction
Triangle & Parallelogram Inequalities
$\|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|$ (triangle inequality)
$\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2(\|\mathbf{u}\|^2+\|\mathbf{v}\|^2)$ (parallelogram law, Ex 4.2.34)
Theorem 4.1.6 — Pythagoras
Right triangle with hypotenuse c and legs a, b: a² + b² = c².
Proof uses similar-triangle ratios; gives the 3-D distance formula via two applications.
Geometry Proofs via Vectors (Ex. 4.1.2 / 4.1.6)
Diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other — proved using tip-to-tail rule alone.
Midpoints of the four sides of any quadrilateral form a parallelogram.
§4.2 Dot Product
Definitions 4.4–4.7 · Theorems 4.2.1–4.2.5
Dot Product (Scalar Product)
Definition 4.4 — Dot Product in R³
For $\mathbf{v} = (x_1,y_1,z_1)^\top$, $\mathbf{w} = (x_2,y_2,z_2)^\top$ the dot product is:
Component Form $$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = x_1x_2 + y_1y_2 + z_1z_2 = \mathbf{v}^\top\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}$$
Theorem 4.2.1 — Properties ($\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, scalar $k$)
#PropertyFormula
1Scalar value$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}$
2Symmetry$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{v}$
3Zero$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{0} = 0$
4Self-dot = ‖·‖²$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$
5Scalar assoc.$(k\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{w} = k(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w})$
6Distributivity$\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v} \pm \mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} \pm \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w}$
Theorem 4.2.2 — Dot Product & Angle
For nonzero v, w with angle $\theta \in [0, \pi]$:
Intrinsic / Geometric Form $$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = \|\mathbf{v}\|\|\mathbf{w}\|\cos\theta$$ $$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|\|\mathbf{w}\|}$$
Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality (Ex. 4.2.44)
Since $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$: $|\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w}| \leq \|\mathbf{v}\|\|\mathbf{w}\|$.
Equality iff v and w are parallel ($\theta = 0$ or $\pi$).
Example 4.2.1 / 4.2.2
$\mathbf{v}=(2,-1,3)^\top$, $\mathbf{w}=(1,4,-1)^\top$: $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = 2-4-3 =$ −5.
$\|\mathbf{v} - 3\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{v}\|^2-6(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w})+9\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$ (expand with Thm 4.2.1).
Angles & Orthogonality
Sign of Dot Product vs. Angle
$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} > 0 \iff \theta$ is acute $(0 \leq \theta < \pi/2)$
$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} < 0 \iff \theta$ is obtuse $(\pi/2 < \theta \leq \pi)$
$\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = 0 \iff \theta = \pi/2$ (perpendicular)
Definition 4.5 — Orthogonality
v and w are orthogonal (written $\mathbf{v} \perp \mathbf{w}$) if v = 0, or w = 0, or the angle between them is $\pi/2$.
Theorem 4.2.3
v and w are orthogonal  ⟺  $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w} = 0$.
Pythagorean Theorem — Dot Product Form (Ex. 4.2.37)
u ⊥ v implies $$\|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$$
Orthogonal Sets & Orthonormal Sets
A set of vectors is orthogonal if every pair is orthogonal. It is orthonormal if additionally every vector is a unit vector. Pairwise orthogonal nonzero vectors are automatically linearly independent.
Example 4.2.3 — Computing θ
$\mathbf{u} = (-1,1,2)^\top$, $\mathbf{v} = (2,1,-1)^\top$:
$\cos\theta = (-2+1-2)/(\sqrt{6}\cdot\sqrt{6}) = -1/2 \implies$ $\theta = 2\pi/3$.
Example 4.2.5 — Rhombus Diagonals ⊥
Adjacent sides u, v with $\|\mathbf{u}\| = \|\mathbf{v}\|$ (rhombus):
$(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2-\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 0 \implies$ diagonals perpendicular.
Projections onto a Vector
Definition 4.6 — Projection of u onto d
Given u and nonzero d, the projection $\mathbf{u}_1 = \text{proj}_{\mathbf{d}}(\mathbf{u})$ is the unique vector parallel to d such that $\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_1 \perp \mathbf{d}$.
Theorem 4.2.4 — Projection Formula
Projection onto d $$\mathbf{u}_1 = \text{proj}_{\mathbf{d}}(\mathbf{u}) = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{d}}{\|\mathbf{d}\|^2}\,\mathbf{d} = (\mathbf{u}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{d}})\,\hat{\mathbf{d}} \quad \text{where } \hat{\mathbf{d}} = \mathbf{d}/\|\mathbf{d}\|$$ $$\mathbf{u}_2 = \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_1 \quad \text{is orthogonal to } \mathbf{d}$$ $$\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{u}_2 \quad \text{(parallel } \oplus \text{ orthogonal decomposition)}$$
Proof Idea
Set $\mathbf{u}_1 = t\mathbf{d}$. Require $(\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{d})\cdot\mathbf{d} = 0 \to \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{d} = t\|\mathbf{d}\|^2 \to$ $t = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{d}/\|\mathbf{d}\|^2$.
Pythagoras in Decomposition
$\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}_1\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}_2\|^2$ since $\mathbf{u}_1 \perp \mathbf{u}_2$. $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{d}}(\mathbf{u})$ is the closest point on $\text{span}\{\mathbf{d}\}$ to the tip of $\mathbf{u}$.
Example 4.2.7 — Decompose u along d
$\mathbf{u} = (2,-3,1)^\top$, $\mathbf{d} = (1,-1,3)^\top$:
$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{d} = 2+3+3=8$, $\|\mathbf{d}\|^2=11$
$\mathbf{u}_1 = (8/11)(1,-1,3)^\top$
$\mathbf{u}_2 = \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_1 = (1/11)(14,-25,-13)^\top$ (verify $\mathbf{u}_2\cdot\mathbf{d} = 0$ ✓)
Example 4.2.6 — Physics: Block on Incline
10 kg block on 30° incline (no friction). Weight w split into:
w₁ ∥ surface (component causing slide), w₂ ⊥ surface.
$\|\mathbf{w}_1\| = \|\mathbf{w}\|\sin 30° = 5$ kg·wt → required restraining force = 5 kg·wt up the slope.
Example 4.2.8 — Point to Line Distance
$P(1,3,-2)$; line through $P_0(2,0,-1)$, $\mathbf{d} = (1,-1,0)^\top$:
$\mathbf{u} = \overrightarrow{P_0P} = (-1,3,-1)^\top$, $\mathbf{u}_1 = \text{proj}_{\mathbf{d}}(\mathbf{u}) = -2\mathbf{d} = (-2,2,0)^\top$
$\text{dist} = \|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_1\| = \|(1,1,-1)^\top\| =$ $\sqrt{3}$.
Closest point $Q = P_0 + \mathbf{u}_1 = (0,2,-1)$.
§4.3 Cross Product
Definition 4.8 · Theorems 4.2.5, 4.3.1–4.3.5 · Lagrange Identity
Cross Product in R³
Definition 4.8 — Cross Product (Vector Product)
For v₁ = (x₁,y₁,z₁)ᵀ, v₂ = (x₂,y₂,z₂)ᵀ the cross product v₁ × v₂ is:
Determinant Form (expanded along column 1) $$\mathbf{v}_1 \times \mathbf{v}_2 = \det\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ x_1 & y_1 & z_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & z_2\end{vmatrix}$$ $$= (y_1z_2 - z_1y_2)\,\mathbf{i} - (x_1z_2 - z_1x_2)\,\mathbf{j} + (x_1y_2 - y_1x_2)\,\mathbf{k}$$
Coordinate Vector Products
i × j = k, j × k = i, k × i = j (cyclic; reverse order gives negatives)
Theorem 4.3.4 — Magnitude & Area
If $\theta$ is the angle between nonzero u, v:
Magnitude of Cross Product $$\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\| = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\sin\theta$$ $=$ area of parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ $$\text{Triangle area} = \tfrac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\|$$
Right-Hand Rule (Direction of u×v)
Curl right-hand fingers from u toward v through angle $\theta$; thumb points in the direction of $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$. Requires a right-hand coordinate system.
Example 4.2.12
$\mathbf{v} = (2,-1,4)^\top$, $\mathbf{w} = (1,3,7)^\top$:
$\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w} = (-7-12)$i $-(14-4)$j $+(6+1)$k $=$ $(-19,-10,7)^\top$
Example 4.3.1 — Triangle Area
$P(2,1,0)$, $Q(3,-1,1)$, $R(1,0,1)$: $\overrightarrow{RP}=(1,1,-1)^\top$, $\overrightarrow{RQ}=(2,-1,0)^\top$.
$\overrightarrow{RP}\times\overrightarrow{RQ} = (-1,-2,-3)^\top$. Area $= \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{1+4+9} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{14}$.
Cross Product Properties
Theorem 4.3.2 — Algebraic Properties ($\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, $k$ scalar)
#PropertyFormula
2Orthogonality$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} \perp \mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} \perp \mathbf{v}$
3Zero factor$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}\times\mathbf{u}$
4Self-product$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}$
5Anti-commutativity$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = -(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u})$
6Scalar$(k\mathbf{u})\times\mathbf{v} = k(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}\times(k\mathbf{v})$
7Left distributive$\mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}) = (\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})+(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{w})$
8Right distributive$(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})\times\mathbf{u} = (\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u})+(\mathbf{w}\times\mathbf{u})$
NOT Associative
In general $(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\times\mathbf{w} \neq \mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w})$. (Verified by counterexample in Exercise 4.3.2.)
Theorem 4.2.5 / 4.3.4 — Parallelism Test
Nonzero u and v are parallel if and only if $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$.
(Contrast: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0 \iff$ orthogonal.)
Theorem 4.3.3 — Lagrange Identity
For any u, v $\in \mathbb{R}^3$:
Lagrange Identity $$\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2$$
Derivation of Magnitude Formula
Substitute $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$ into the Lagrange identity:
$\|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2(1-\cos^2\theta) = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2\sin^2\theta$,
then take the positive square root ($\sin\theta \geq 0$ for $\theta \in [0,\pi]$).
Scalar Triple Product & Parallelepiped Volume
Theorem 4.3.1 — Scalar Triple Product
For u, v, w $\in \mathbb{R}^3$:
Determinant Form $$\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}) = \det[\,\mathbf{u}\;\mathbf{v}\;\mathbf{w}\,]$$ (matrix with $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$ as columns)
Orthogonality Consequence
Setting u = v (or u = w) gives two identical columns → det = 0 → $\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w} \perp \mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w} \perp \mathbf{w}$ (reproves Thm 4.2.5(1)).
Theorem 4.3.5 — Parallelepiped Volume
Volume Formula $$\text{Vol}(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{w}\cdot(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})| = |\det[\,\mathbf{u}\;\mathbf{v}\;\mathbf{w}\,]|$$ $$\text{Triangle area} = \tfrac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}\|$$ $$\text{Pyramid volume} = \tfrac{1}{6}\left|\overrightarrow{PQ}\cdot\!\left(\overrightarrow{PR}\times\overrightarrow{PS}\right)\right| \quad \text{(Ex 4.3.20)}$$
Coplanarity Test
Three vectors (or four points) are coplanar iff:
$$\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}) = 0 \quad \text{(equivalently, } \det[\,\mathbf{u}\;\mathbf{v}\;\mathbf{w}\,] = 0\text{)}$$
Example 4.3.2 — Parallelepiped Volume
$\mathbf{w} = (1,2,-1)^\top$, $\mathbf{u} = (1,1,0)^\top$, $\mathbf{v} = (-2,0,1)^\top$:
$\det[\mathbf{u}\;\mathbf{v}\;\mathbf{w}] = -3 \to$ Volume $= |-3| =$ 3.
Example 4.2.13 — Plane through 3 Points
$P(1,3,-2)$, $Q(1,1,5)$, $R(2,-2,3)$:
$\overrightarrow{PQ}=(0,-2,7)^\top$, $\overrightarrow{PR}=(1,-5,5)^\top$
n $= \overrightarrow{PQ}\times\overrightarrow{PR} = (25,7,2)^\top \to$ plane 25x+7y+2z = 42.
Example 4.2.14 — Skew-Line Distance
Lines with $\mathbf{d}_1=(2,0,1)^\top$, $\mathbf{d}_2=(1,1,-1)^\top$ through $P_1(1,0,-1)$ and $P_2(3,1,0)$:
$\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{d}_1\times\mathbf{d}_2 = (-1,3,2)^\top$, $\mathbf{u} = \overrightarrow{P_1P_2} = (2,1,1)^\top$
$\text{dist} = |\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{n}|/\|\mathbf{n}\| = 3/\sqrt{14} = 3\sqrt{14}/14$.
§4.1–§4.2 Lines and Planes in R³
Definition 4.3, 4.7 · Scalar / Vector equations · Distance formulas
Lines in R³
Definition 4.3 — Direction Vector
A nonzero vector $\mathbf{d}$ is a direction vector of a line if it is parallel to $\overrightarrow{AB}$ for some two distinct points A, B on the line.
Vector & Parametric Equations of a Line
Line through $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ with direction $\mathbf{d} = (a,b,c)^\top \neq \mathbf{0}$:
Vector Form $$\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{p}_0 + t\,\mathbf{d}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$
Parametric Equations $$x = x_0 + ta$$ $$y = y_0 + tb \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}$$ $$z = z_0 + tc$$
Symmetric Form (a, b, c all nonzero, Exercise 4.1.27)
$$\frac{x-x_0}{a} = \frac{y-y_0}{b} = \frac{z-z_0}{c}$$
Line Through Two Points
Use $\mathbf{d} = P_1 - P_0$ as direction vector. Changing the base point $P_0$ only shifts the parameter.
Example 4.1.8
Through $P_0(2,0,1)$ and $P_1(4,-1,1)$: d $= \overrightarrow{P_0P_1} = (2,-1,0)^\top$.
Parametric: $x = 2+2t$, $y = -t$, $z = 1$.
Example 4.1.10 — Intersection of Two Lines
Set $\mathbf{p}_0+t\mathbf{d}_1 = \mathbf{p}_1+s\mathbf{d}_2$ component-wise; solve the $3\times 2$ system for $t$, $s$.
Lines intersect iff the system is consistent.
Planes in R³
Definition 4.7 — Normal Vector
A nonzero $\mathbf{n}$ is a normal for a plane if it is orthogonal to every vector in the plane. $\mathbf{k} = (0,0,1)^\top$ is normal to the x-y plane.
Scalar & Vector Equations
Plane through $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ with normal $\mathbf{n} = (a,b,c)^\top \neq \mathbf{0}$:
Scalar Form $$a(x-x_0) + b(y-y_0) + c(z-z_0) = 0$$ General form: $ax + by + cz = d$ where $d = ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0$
Vector Form $$\mathbf{n}\cdot(\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{p}_0) = 0 \iff \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{p} = d$$
Plane through 3 Non-collinear Points
Given $P_0$, $P_1$, $P_2$ (not all on one line):
$$\mathbf{n} = (\mathbf{P}_1-\mathbf{P}_0) \times (\mathbf{P}_2-\mathbf{P}_0) \quad \text{(normal via cross product)}$$
Then use $P_0$ and n in the scalar equation.
Example 4.2.9
$P_0(1,-1,3)$, n $= (3,-1,2)^\top$:
$3(x-1) - (y+1) + 2(z-3) = 0 \to$ 3x − y + 2z = 10.
Example 4.2.10 — Parallel Planes
Plane parallel to $2x-3y = 6$ through $P_0(3,-1,2)$:
Same normal $\mathbf{n}=(2,-3,0)^\top \to 2x-3y = d$; $d = 2(3)-3(-1) = 9$.
Equation: 2x − 3y = 9.
Distances in R³
Point to Plane (Example 4.2.11 / Exercise 4.3.6)
Plane $ax+by+cz = d$, point $P_0$ with vector $\mathbf{p}_0$:
Point–Plane Distance $$\text{dist} = \frac{|\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{p}_0 - d|}{\|\mathbf{n}\|} = \frac{|ax_0+by_0+cz_0-d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$$ Closest point $Q$: $\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{p}_0 + t\,\mathbf{n}$, $t = \dfrac{d - \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{p}_0}{\|\mathbf{n}\|^2}$
Point to Line (Example 4.2.8 / Exercise 4.3.8)
Line through $P_0$ with direction d, point $P$ with $\overrightarrow{P_0P} = \mathbf{u}$:
Point–Line Distance $$\text{dist} = \|\mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{d}}(\mathbf{u})\| = \frac{\|\overrightarrow{P_0P} \times \mathbf{d}\|}{\|\mathbf{d}\|}$$
Parallel Planes (Exercise 4.3.22)
Planes $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{p} = d_1$ and $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{p} = d_2$ (same normal n):
Between Parallel Planes $$\text{dist} = \frac{|d_1 - d_2|}{\|\mathbf{n}\|}$$
Skew (Non-parallel, Non-intersecting) Lines (Example 4.2.14)
Lines through $P_1$, $P_2$ with directions $\mathbf{d}_1$, $\mathbf{d}_2$:
Skew-Line Distance $$\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{d}_1 \times \mathbf{d}_2 \quad \text{(common perpendicular direction)}$$ $$\text{dist} = \frac{|\overrightarrow{P_1P_2} \cdot \mathbf{n}|}{\|\mathbf{n}\|}$$
Direction Cosines (Exercise 4.2.41)
Angles $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$ that $\mathbf{v} = (a,b,c)^\top$ makes with x, y, z axes:
$\cos\alpha = a/\|\mathbf{v}\|$, $\cos\beta = b/\|\mathbf{v}\|$, $\cos\gamma = c/\|\mathbf{v}\|$.
$\cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1$.
Linear Combinations & Geometry
Midpoint Formula (Example 4.1.5)
Point $M$ midway between $P_1$ and $P_2$ (vectors $\mathbf{p}_1$, $\mathbf{p}_2$):
Midpoint $$\mathbf{m} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{p}_1 + \mathbf{p}_2) \quad \text{(average of position vectors)}$$
Weighted Division (Exercise 4.1.16)
Point $P$ lying $r/(r+s)$ of the way from $P_1$ to $P_2$:
$$\mathbf{p} = \frac{s}{r+s}\,\mathbf{p}_1 + \frac{r}{r+s}\,\mathbf{p}_2$$
Special case $r=s=1$: midpoint; $r=1$, $s=2$: one-third from $P_1$.
Centroid of a Triangle (Exercise 4.1.34)
Centroid / Centre of Mass $$G = \tfrac{1}{3}(\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{q} + \mathbf{r}) \quad \text{(lies on all three medians)}$$
Collinearity Test
Points $P$, $Q$, $R$ are collinear iff:
$$\overrightarrow{PQ} \times \overrightarrow{PR} = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{(cross product form, Exercise 4.3.10)}$$ $\iff R = P + t(Q - P)$ for some scalar $t$
Coplanarity Test (Four Points)
Points $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ are coplanar iff:
$$\overrightarrow{AB}\cdot(\overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC}) = 0 \quad \text{(scalar triple product = 0, Exercise 4.3.11)}$$
Example 4.1.5 / 4.1.6 — Midpoints
$M$ one-third of the way from $P_1$ to $P_2$:
$\mathbf{m} = (2/3)\mathbf{p}_1 + (1/3)\mathbf{p}_2 = \mathbf{p}_1 + (1/3)(\mathbf{p}_2 - \mathbf{p}_1)$.
Midpoints of the four sides of any quadrilateral form a parallelogram.
Example 4.1.2 — Parallelogram Diagonals Bisect
Using tip-to-tail: $\overrightarrow{AM} = \overrightarrow{MC}$ (midpoint $M$ of $AC$) $\implies \overrightarrow{BM} = -\overrightarrow{MD}$
$\implies M$ is also the midpoint of $BD$. Pure vector proof, no coordinates.
