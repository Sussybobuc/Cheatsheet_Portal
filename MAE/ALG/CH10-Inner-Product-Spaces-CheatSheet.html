§10.1 Inner Products and Norms
Inner Product Definition (Definition 10.1)
Definition 10.1
An inner product on a real vector space $V$ is a function assigning a real number $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle$ to every pair $v, w \in V$ satisfying:
Axioms IP1–IP4 $$\text{IP1.}\quad \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u} \rangle \qquad [\text{symmetry}]$$ $$\text{IP2.}\quad \langle \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle \qquad [\text{additivity in 1st arg}]$$ $$\text{IP3.}\quad \langle c\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = c\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \quad \text{for all } c \in \mathbb{R} \qquad [\text{homogeneity in 1st arg}]$$ $$\text{IP4.}\quad \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \geq 0;\quad \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0 \iff \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \qquad [\text{positive definiteness}]$$
Theorem 10.1.1 — Basic Properties
For all $u, v, w \in V$ and $r \in \mathbb{R}$:
(1) $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{u},\mathbf{v} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{u},\mathbf{w} \rangle$   (2) $\langle \mathbf{v}, r\mathbf{w} \rangle = r\langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle$
(3) $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{0} \rangle = 0 = \langle \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$   (4) $\langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$ if and only if $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$
Linearity in first argument
IP2 + IP3 together give full linearity in the first argument: $\langle r_1\mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + r_n\mathbf{v}_n, \mathbf{w} \rangle = r_1\langle \mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{w} \rangle + \cdots + r_n\langle \mathbf{v}_n,\mathbf{w} \rangle$. An inner product space (IPS) is a vector space $V$ with a chosen inner product. Every subspace of an IPS is again an IPS with the same inner product.
Examples of Inner Products
Example 10.1.1 — $\mathbb{R}^n$ standard (euclidean)
$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + \cdots + u_nv_n$. The euclidean inner product; $\mathbb{R}^n$ with it is euclidean $n$-space.
Example 10.1.2 — Matrix space Mmn
For $m\times n$ matrices $A$, $B$: $\langle A, B \rangle = \text{tr}(AB^T) = \sum_{i,j} a_{ij}b_{ij}$.
Example 10.1.3 — C[a, b] (function space)
For continuous $f$, $g$ on $[a, b]$: $\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x)g(x)\,dx$.
Weighted inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$ (Ex. 10.1.5)
$\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle = a_1v_1w_1 + a_2v_2w_2 + \cdots + a_nv_nw_n$ with $a_i > 0$.
Theorem 10.1.2 — Positive Definite Matrix Inner Products
If $A$ is any $n\times n$ positive definite matrix, $\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{y}$ defines an inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$, and every inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$ arises this way.
§10.1 Norm and Distance
Induced Norm (Definition 10.2)
Definition 10.2
The induced norm of $\mathbf{v} \in V$ is $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}$. The distance is $d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\|$. $\mathbf{v}$ is a unit vector if $\|\mathbf{v}\| = 1$; $\mathbf{v}/\|\mathbf{v}\|$ is the unique positive-multiple unit vector (Thm 10.1.3).
Theorem 10.1.5 — Norm Properties $$(1)\quad \|\mathbf{v}\| \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{v} \in V$$ $$(2)\quad \|\mathbf{v}\| = 0 \iff \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$ $$(3)\quad \|c\mathbf{v}\| = |c|\|\mathbf{v}\| \quad \text{(homogeneity)}$$ $$(4)\quad \|\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\| \leq \|\mathbf{v}\| + \|\mathbf{w}\| \quad \text{(triangle inequality)}$$
Theorem 10.1.6 — Distance Properties
(1) $d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) \geq 0$   (2) $d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) = 0$ iff $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w}$
(3) $d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) = d(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{v})$   (4) $d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}) \leq d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}) + d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w})$
Example 10.1.5 — Function norm
In $C[a,b]$: $\|f\| = \sqrt{\int_a^b f(x)^2\,dx}$. $\|f\|^2$ equals the area beneath the graph of $y = f(x)^2$.
Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality (Theorem 10.1.4)
Theorem 10.1.4 — Cauchy-Schwarz
For any $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$ in an inner product space $V$:
$\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle^2 \leq \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 \|\mathbf{w}\|^2$
Equivalently: $|\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{v}\| \|\mathbf{w}\|$.
Equality holds iff one of $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$ is a scalar multiple of the other (parallel).
Proof sketch — let $a = \|\mathbf{v}\|$, $b = \|\mathbf{w}\|$ $$\|b\mathbf{v} - a\mathbf{w}\|^2 = 2ab(ab - \langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle) \geq 0$$ $$\|b\mathbf{v} + a\mathbf{w}\|^2 = 2ab(ab + \langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle) \geq 0$$ $$\Rightarrow\; -ab \leq \langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle \leq ab \;\Rightarrow\; |\langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{v}\|\|\mathbf{w}\|$$
Angle between vectors (Exercise 10.1.31)
Cauchy-Schwarz guarantees $|\langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle|/(\|\mathbf{v}\|\|\mathbf{w}\|) \in [0, 1]$, so the angle $\theta$ between $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$ is well-defined: $\cos\theta = \langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle/(\|\mathbf{v}\|\|\mathbf{w}\|)$, where $0 \leq \theta \leq \pi$.
Consequence: triangle inequality $\|\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\| \leq \|\mathbf{v}\| + \|\mathbf{w}\|$ (Theorem 10.1.5 (4)).
Example 10.1.8 — Integral form
For $f, g \in C[a,b]$: $$\left(\int_a^b fg\,dx\right)^2 \leq \left(\int_a^b f^2\,dx\right)\left(\int_a^b g^2\,dx\right)$$
Parallelogram Law & Pythagorean Theorem
Parallelogram Law $$\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|^2 = 2\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$$
Holds in every inner product space
Sum of squared diagonals equals twice the sum of squared sides of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
Polarisation identity: $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \tfrac{1}{4}(\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 - \|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|^2)$ — recovers inner product from the norm.
Exercise 10.1.23: $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(\|\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w}\|^2)$.
Theorem 10.2.1 — Pythagorean Theorem
If $\{f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_n\}$ is an orthogonal set:
$\|f_1 + f_2 + \cdots + f_n\|^2 = \|f_1\|^2 + \|f_2\|^2 + \cdots + \|f_n\|^2$
In particular: $\|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$ iff $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$.
§10.2 Orthogonal Sets of Vectors
Orthogonality (§10.2)
Definition
$\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$ iff $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$. A set $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ is orthogonal if each $\mathbf{v}_i \neq \mathbf{0}$ and $\langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j \rangle = 0$ for all $i \neq j$. It is orthonormal if additionally $\|\mathbf{v}_i\| = 1$ for each $i$, i.e., $\langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$.
Theorem 10.2.3 — Orthogonal $\Rightarrow$ Linearly Independent
Every orthogonal (nonzero) set of vectors is linearly independent.
Theorem 10.2.6 — Orthogonal Complement
$U^\perp = \{ \mathbf{v} \in V \mid \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u} \rangle = 0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{u} \in U \}$ is a subspace of $V$. If $U$ is finite-dimensional:
(1) $V = U \oplus U^\perp$   (2) $\dim U + \dim U^\perp = \dim V$   (3) $(U^\perp)^\perp = U$
Example 10.2.1 — Trig functions
In $C[-\pi, \pi]$: $\langle \sin x, \cos x \rangle = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin x \cos x\,dx = 0$. So $\{\sin x, \cos x\}$ is an orthogonal set.
Normalising (Theorem 10.2.2)
Replace each $f_i$ by $f_i/\|f_i\|$ to convert an orthogonal set into an orthonormal set.
Orthogonal Projection (Definition 10.3, Theorem 10.2.7)
Definition 10.3
The orthogonal projection onto a finite-dimensional subspace $U$ is the linear operator $\text{proj}_U: V \to V$ with image $U$ and kernel $U^\perp$.
Theorem 10.2.7 — Projection Formula Given any orthonormal basis $\{f_1,\ldots,f_k\}$ for $U \subseteq V$: $$\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) = \langle \mathbf{v},f_1 \rangle f_1 + \langle \mathbf{v},f_2 \rangle f_2 + \cdots + \langle \mathbf{v},f_k \rangle f_k$$ If $\{f_1,\ldots,f_k\}$ is only orthogonal ($\|f_i\|$ not necessarily 1): $$\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) = \sum_{i} \frac{\langle \mathbf{v},f_i \rangle}{\|f_i\|^2} f_i$$
Theorem 10.2.4 — Expansion Theorem
Let $\{f_1, \ldots, f_n\}$ be an orthogonal basis of $V$. For any $\mathbf{v} \in V$:
$\mathbf{v} = \sum_i \frac{\langle \mathbf{v},f_i \rangle}{\|f_i\|^2} f_i$. The coefficients $\langle \mathbf{v},f_i \rangle/\|f_i\|^2$ are the Fourier coefficients of $\mathbf{v}$ w.r.t. the orthogonal basis.
Key decomposition
Every $\mathbf{v}$ decomposes uniquely as $\mathbf{v} = \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) + (\mathbf{v} - \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}))$, where $\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) \in U$ and $\mathbf{v} - \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) \in U^\perp$.
Pythagorean: $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v} - \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\|^2$
§10.2 Gram-Schmidt & Best Approximation
Gram-Schmidt Process, Abstract (Theorem 10.2.5)
Theorem 10.2.5 — Gram-Schmidt Algorithm
Given any linearly independent $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ in an IPS $V$:
Gram-Schmidt recurrence (produces ONB) $$f_1 = \frac{\mathbf{v}_1}{\|\mathbf{v}_1\|}$$ For $k \geq 2$: $$y_k = \mathbf{v}_k - \langle \mathbf{v}_k,f_1 \rangle f_1 - \langle \mathbf{v}_k,f_2 \rangle f_2 - \cdots - \langle \mathbf{v}_k,f_{k-1} \rangle f_{k-1}$$ $$f_k = \frac{y_k}{\|y_k\|}$$
Result
$\{f_1, \ldots, f_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $\text{span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$. $\text{span}\{f_1,\ldots,f_j\} = \text{span}\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_j\}$ for each $j = 1,\ldots,k$. Works in any IPS; uses the specific inner product $\langle \cdot,\cdot \rangle$ of that space.
Example 10.2.4 — Legendre Polynomials
On $P_3$ with $\langle p,q \rangle = \int_{-1}^{1} p(x)q(x)\,dx$, GS on $\{1, x, x^2, x^3\}$ yields: $\{1,\; x,\; \tfrac{1}{3}(3x^2-1),\; \tfrac{1}{5}(5x^3-3x)\}$ — the first four Legendre polynomials.
Corollary 10.2.1 — IPS Isomorphism
Every $n$-dimensional IPS is isomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ as an inner product space. If $E = \{f_1,\ldots,f_n\}$ is any ONB of $V$, then $C_E: V \to \mathbb{R}^n$ satisfies $\langle \mathbf{v},\mathbf{w} \rangle = C_E(\mathbf{v})\cdot C_E(\mathbf{w})$.
Best Approximation Theorem (Theorem 10.2.8)
Theorem 10.2.8 — Best Approximation
Let $U$ be a finite-dimensional subspace of an IPS $V$. For any $\mathbf{v} \in V$:
$\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})$ is the UNIQUE closest vector in $U$ to $\mathbf{v}$.
$\|\mathbf{v} - \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\| < \|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}\|$ for all $\mathbf{u} \in U$, $\mathbf{u} \neq \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})$.
Equivalently: $\|\mathbf{v} - \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\| = \min_{\mathbf{u}\in U} \|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}\|$.
Proof via Pythagorean Theorem Write $p = \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})$. For any $\mathbf{u} \in U$, $\mathbf{u} \neq p$: $$\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u} = (\mathbf{v} - p) + (p - \mathbf{u})$$ $\mathbf{v} - p \in U^\perp$ and $p - \mathbf{u} \in U$ $\Rightarrow$ they are orthogonal $$\|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{v} - p\|^2 + \|p - \mathbf{u}\|^2 > \|\mathbf{v} - p\|^2$$
Key principle
$\mathbf{v} - \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) \in U^\perp$ — the error vector is always perpendicular to $U$. As $U$ grows ($U_1 \subseteq U_2 \subseteq \cdots$), the approximation error decreases: $\|\mathbf{v}-\text{proj}_{U_2}(\mathbf{v})\| \leq \|\mathbf{v}-\text{proj}_{U_1}(\mathbf{v})\|$.
Example 10.2.6 — Polynomial best approximation
Best degree-$\leq 2$ approximation to $|x|$ on $[-1,1]$ in $C[-1,1]$: $p(x) = \text{proj}_{P_2}(|x|) = \tfrac{3}{16}(5x^2 + 1)$. Computed using orthogonal basis $\{1, x, 3x^2-1\}$ of $P_2$.
§10.5 An Application to Fourier Approximation
Fourier Series Approximation (Theorem 10.5.1)
Setting — $C[-\pi, \pi]$ with $\langle f,g \rangle = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f\cdot g\,dx$
The set $\{1, \sin x, \cos x, \sin 2x, \cos 2x, \ldots\}$ is orthogonal in $C[-\pi, \pi]$: $\|1\|^2 = 2\pi$,   $\|\sin kx\|^2 = \pi$,   $\|\cos kx\|^2 = \pi$ for $k \geq 1$. ONB: $\{1/\sqrt{2\pi},\; \cos x/\sqrt{\pi},\; \sin x/\sqrt{\pi},\; \cos 2x/\sqrt{\pi},\; \sin 2x/\sqrt{\pi},\; \ldots\}$.
Fourier coefficients of $f \in C[-\pi, \pi]$ $$a_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\,dx$$ $$a_k = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos(kx)\,dx, \quad k \geq 1$$ $$b_k = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin(kx)\,dx, \quad k \geq 1$$
Theorem 10.5.1 — $n$-th Fourier Approximation
Let $F_n = \text{span}\{1, \cos x, \sin x, \ldots, \cos nx, \sin nx\}$. The unique closest function in $F_n$ to $f$ is:
$f_n(x) = a_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{n} [a_k\cos(kx) + b_k\sin(kx)] = \text{proj}_{F_n}(f)$.
Theorem 10.5.2: for continuous $f$, $f_n(x) \to f(x)$ for all $x \in (-\pi, \pi)$.
Example 10.5.2 — Leibniz formula
$f(x) = x$ on $[-\pi,\pi]$ is odd $\Rightarrow$ $a_k = 0$. Fourier series: $x = 2[\sin x - \sin(2x)/2 + \sin(3x)/3 - \cdots]$. Setting $x = \pi/2$: $\pi/4 = 1 - 1/3 + 1/5 - 1/7 + \cdots$
Even / Odd simplification
$f$ even $\Rightarrow$ $b_k = 0$ (all sine coefficients vanish). $f$ odd $\Rightarrow$ $a_k = 0$ (all cosine coefficients vanish). $F_1 \subseteq F_2 \subseteq \cdots$ $\Rightarrow$ errors decrease: $\|f-f_1\| \geq \|f-f_2\| \geq \cdots$
Bessel's Inequality
Bessel's Inequality (Exercise 10.2.23)
Let $\{f_1, \ldots, f_m\}$ be any orthogonal set in an IPS $V$, $U = \text{span}\{f_1,\ldots,f_m\}$. For any $\mathbf{v} \in V$:
Bessel's inequality $$\|\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\|^2 = \frac{\langle \mathbf{v},f_1 \rangle^2}{\|f_1\|^2} + \cdots + \frac{\langle \mathbf{v},f_m \rangle^2}{\|f_m\|^2} \leq \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$$ If $\{f_1,\ldots,f_m\}$ is orthonormal: $$\langle \mathbf{v},f_1 \rangle^2 + \langle \mathbf{v},f_2 \rangle^2 + \cdots + \langle \mathbf{v},f_m \rangle^2 \leq \|\mathbf{v}\|^2$$
Equality — Parseval's Identity (Exercise 10.2.22)
Equality holds iff $\mathbf{v} \in U$. When $\{f_1,\ldots,f_n\}$ is an ONB for all of $V$:
$\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \langle \mathbf{v},f_1 \rangle^2 + \langle \mathbf{v},f_2 \rangle^2 + \cdots + \langle \mathbf{v},f_n \rangle^2$ (Parseval's identity)
Measures how much of $\mathbf{v}$ is captured in the subspace $U$; equality iff $\mathbf{v} \in U$.
Proof sketch
$\mathbf{v} = \text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) + (\mathbf{v}-\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}))$ with $\mathbf{v}-\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v}) \in U^\perp$. Pythagoras gives $\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}-\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\|^2 \geq \|\text{proj}_U(\mathbf{v})\|^2$.
Gram Matrix
Definition — Gram Matrix
Given $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ in an IPS $V$, the Gram matrix $G$ is the $k\times k$ matrix:
$G_{ij} = \langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j \rangle$
Standard dot-product case If $A = [\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \mathbf{v}_2 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_k]$ (columns $\mathbf{v}_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$): $$G = A^T A$$
Symmetry & Positive Semidefiniteness
$G$ is always symmetric (by symmetry of $\langle \cdot,\cdot \rangle$) and positive semidefinite: $\mathbf{x}^T G\mathbf{x} = \langle \sum x_i\mathbf{v}_i, \sum x_i\mathbf{v}_i \rangle = \|\sum x_i\mathbf{v}_i\|^2 \geq 0$.
Key Property — Linear Independence
$\det(G) \neq 0$  iff  $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ is linearly independent.
Equivalently, $G$ is positive definite iff the $\mathbf{v}_i$ are linearly independent.
Standard case: $\det(A^T A) \neq 0$ iff columns of $A$ are linearly independent.
Applications
  • Gram-Schmidt produces $G = I$ when $\{\mathbf{v}_i\}$ is already orthonormal
  • Volume: $\sqrt{\det(G)}$ = $k$-dimensional volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$
  • $G$ encodes all geometric information (angles, lengths) about $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}$
