§2.1 Area Between Curves
Area — Functions of x
Setup
If f(x) ≥ g(x) on [a, b], the area between the curves is:
Area (vertical slices) $$A = \int_a^b \bigl[f(x)-g(x)\bigr]\,dx$$
When curves cross
Find intersection points, split integral, and use |f − g| on each subinterval.
Example
Area between y = x² and y = x: intersect at x = 0, 1
A = ∫01(x − x²) dx = [x²/2 − x³/3]01 = 1/6
Area — Functions of y
Setup
If integrating with respect to y is easier (e.g., sideways parabolas):
Area (horizontal slices) $$A = \int_c^d \bigl[f(y)-g(y)\bigr]\,dy$$
When to use
Use horizontal slices when boundaries are expressed as x = f(y), or when it avoids splitting the integral at crossing points.
Example
Area between x = y² and x = y+2: intersect at y = −1, 2
A = ∫−12((y+2) − y²) dy = [y²/2 + 2y − y³/3]−12 = 9/2
§2.2–§2.3 Volumes by Slicing & Revolution
Volume by Slicing (General)
Principle
If A(x) is the cross-sectional area at position x, then:
General Volume by Slicing $$V = \int_a^b A(x)\,dx$$
  • Square cross-sections: A = s²
  • Circular cross-sections: A = πr²
  • Equilateral triangle: A = (√3/4)s²
  • Semicircle: A = πr²/2
Tip
Identify the variable of integration by orienting the solid along that axis.
Disk & Washer Methods
Disk Method (solid revolution, no hole)
Rotate f(x) about the x-axis:
Disk Method $$V = \pi\int_a^b \bigl[f(x)\bigr]^2\,dx$$
Washer Method (revolution with hole)
Rotate region between f(x) ≥ g(x) ≥ 0 about x-axis:
Washer Method $$V = \pi\int_a^b \Bigl(\bigl[f(x)\bigr]^2 - \bigl[g(x)\bigr]^2\Bigr)\,dx$$
About y-axis
Replace x with y as the variable of integration; express functions as x = g(y).
Shell Method (Cylindrical Shells)
Concept
Rotate a thin vertical strip (shell) about the y-axis; integrate shells from x = a to x = b.
Shell Method — About y-axis $$V = 2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx \quad (a\geq 0)$$
Shell Method — About x-axis $$V = 2\pi\int_c^d y\,g(y)\,dy \quad (c\geq 0)$$
Shell vs Washer
Use shells when integrating parallel to the axis is easier. Shell: no need to solve for inverse function.
Example
Revolve y = x² (0 ≤ x ≤ 2) about y-axis:
V = 2π ∫02 x · x² dx = 2π [x⁴/4]02 = 8π
Method Selection Guide
AxisDisk/WasherShell
x-axis∫ f(x) dx vertically∫ g(y) dy horizontally
y-axis∫ g(y) dy horizontally∫ f(x) dx vertically
Decision Rule
  • If revolving about y-axis and function given as y = f(x) → use shells
  • If revolving about x-axis and function given as y = f(x) → use disk/washer
  • Choose whichever avoids solving for the inverse or splitting the region
§2.4 Arc Length & §2.5 Surface Area of Revolution
Arc Length Formula
Theorem
If f′ is continuous on [a, b], the arc length of y = f(x) is:
Arc Length — y = f(x) $$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1+[f\'(x)]^2}\,dx$$
Arc Length — x = g(y) $$L = \int_c^d \sqrt{1+[g\'(y)]^2}\,dy$$
Example
Arc length of y = (2/3)x3/2 from 0 to 1:
f′ = x1/2, L = ∫01√(1+x) dx = [(2/3)(1+x)3/2]01 = 2√2/3 − 2/3
Surface Area of Revolution
Theorem
Surface area when y = f(x) ≥ 0 is rotated about the x-axis:
Surface Area — About x-axis $$S = 2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+[f\'(x)]^2}\,dx$$
Surface Area — About y-axis $$S = 2\pi\int_a^b x\sqrt{1+[f\'(x)]^2}\,dx$$
Mnemonic
S = 2π · (distance from axis) · (arc length element)
The 2πr term is the circumference; √(1+[f′]²)dx is the arc length element.
§2.6–§2.7 Physical Applications: Work & Hydrostatic Force
Work
Constant Force $$W = F \cdot d$$
Variable Force $$W = \int_a^b F(x)\,dx$$
Hooke's Law (Spring)
F = kx, where k is the spring constant, x is displacement from natural length.
W = ∫0d kx dx = kd²/2
Pumping Liquid
W = ρg ∫ab A(x) · d(x) dx
where ρ = density, g = gravity, A(x) = cross-section area, d(x) = lift distance for slice at x.
Pumping Unit
Water: ρg = 62.4 lb/ft³ (imperial) or 9800 N/m³ (SI)
Hydrostatic Force & Pressure
Pressure at Depth
Pressure at depth h: P = ρgh (ρ = fluid density)
Hydrostatic Force on Submerged Plate $$F = \rho g\int_a^b h(x)\,w(x)\,dx$$
Variables
h(x) = depth of strip at position x
w(x) = width of strip at position x
Integrate perpendicular to the water surface.
Example Setup
A vertical plate submerged with top edge at depth d₁, bottom at d₂:
F = ρg ∫d₁d₂ x · w(x) dx
§2.8 Moments & Center of Mass
Center of Mass of a Region
Setup
Region R bounded by f(x) ≥ g(x) ≥ 0 on [a, b] with uniform density ρ = 1.
Mass & Moments $$m = \int_a^b [f(x)-g(x)]\,dx$$ $$M_x = \tfrac{1}{2}\int_a^b \bigl([f(x)]^2-[g(x)]^2\bigr)\,dx \qquad M_y = \int_a^b x[f(x)-g(x)]\,dx$$
Centroid (Center of Mass) $$\bar{x} = \frac{M_y}{m} \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{M_x}{m}$$
Pappus's Theorem
Volume of solid of revolution = 2π · ȳ · A (about x-axis)
where ȳ is the y-centroid and A is the area of the region.
Exponential Growth & Decay Models
Model
Exponential growth/decay arises when dy/dt = ky:
General Solution $$y(t) = y_0\,e^{kt}$$
  • k > 0: growth (population, compound interest)
  • k < 0: decay (radioactive decay, cooling)
Half-life / Doubling Time $$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{|k|} \qquad t_d = \frac{\ln 2}{k}\;(k>0)$$
Carbon-14 Dating
k = −0.000121 yr⁻¹ (t½ ≈ 5730 yr)
t = ln(y/y₀) / k = −ln(ratio) / 0.000121
